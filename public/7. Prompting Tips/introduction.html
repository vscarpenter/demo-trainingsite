Copilot prompting tips & tricks

Principles that make prompts work

1) Purpose over prose

State the goal first. Copilot performs best when it knows the outcome you want, not just the topic.

Try: “Create a one‑page executive brief that explains why this matters and what to decide.”

2) Context is king

Reference specific files, tables, or pages. Copilot can’t infer what you didn’t provide.

Attach or cite the source. “Use the Q2_Finance.xlsx sheet ‘Bookings’.”

3) Constraints guide quality

Boundaries like length, tone, structure, or audience keep outputs on target.

“Limit to 200 words, plain language, with 3 bullets for risks.”

4) Iterate like a teammate

Ask for assumptions, request alternatives, and refine quickly rather than trying for a perfect first prompt.

Follow with: “List assumptions you made. What’s missing?”

Reusable anatomy

A reliable prompt structure

G • Goal

  • What do you want? (summary, draft, plan, analysis, slides)
  • Who is it for? (audience & reading level)
“Draft a 1‑page policy summary for non‑technical leaders.”

C • Context

  • Point to sources (files, tables, pages, chat)
  • Call out sections to focus on
“Use the ‘Controls’ section from the SOP + today’s notes.”

K • Constraints

  • Length, format, tone, due date
  • In/Out of scope
“≤ 5 bullets, assertive tone, include owners & due dates.”

S • Style

  • Examples or reference style guides
  • Voice: executive, friendly, analytical
“Write like the Azure Well‑Architected guidance.”

SRC • Sources

  • List what to rely on (and what not to)
  • Ask for citations or links back
“Cite page titles with links back to SharePoint.”

OUT • Output

  • Exact deliverable (table, deck, email, checklist)
  • File format or slide count if needed
“Produce 6 slides with speaker notes and a decision slide.”
Patterns by app →
Quick starts

Common patterns by app

Word — Draft & refine

  • Turn bullets into a memo with headings
  • Rewrite for tone/length
  • Extract actions and risks
“Draft a 1‑page memo from these bullets, plain language, end with ‘Next steps’.”

Excel — Explain & analyze

  • Explain drivers and anomalies
  • Build simple scenarios
  • Create pivots and charts
“Analyze bookings by region (6 quarters). Summarize drivers and flag outliers.”

PowerPoint — Build slides

  • Create deck from a doc
  • Polish titles & speaker notes
  • Version for different audiences
“Create 7 slides from this Word doc, exec audience, one idea/slide.”

Outlook — Triage & reply

  • Summarize threads
  • Draft replies with owners/dates
  • Turn mail into tasks/events
“Summarize this thread in 5 bullets and draft my reply confirming next steps.”

Teams — Meetings & chat

  • Real‑time meeting notes
  • Channel catch‑ups
  • Answer “what did we decide?”
“Create a meeting recap with decisions, owners, and open issues.”

SharePoint — Pages & updates

  • Draft pages from files
  • Summarize SOPs & policies
  • Propose updates
“Draft a landing page summarizing this SOP for frontline staff.”

OneNote — Organize & summarize

  • Summarize pages to actions
  • Restructure brainstorms
  • Create agendas & checklists
“From this page, produce a recap with owners and due dates.”
Refining techniques →
From rough to right

Refining techniques

Assumption check

“List the assumptions you used and questions you still have.”
Quickly correct facts and point to the right sources.

Contrastive edits

“Give me a shorter version (<120 words) and a detailed version (300–400).”

Style swap

“Rewrite for an executive audience, then for frontline staff.”

Structure switch

“Convert this into a table with columns: Owner, Task, Date, Risk.”

Traceability

“Cite sources with links to SharePoint pages and file names.”

Time box

“Produce a 5‑slide version that I can present in 3 minutes.”
Guardrails →
Accuracy & safety

Guardrails to include in prompts

Scope & sources

  • “Use only the attached files and current page.”
  • “Do not invent data; say ‘not found’ if missing.”

Compliance notes

  • “Avoid PII. Redact examples as needed.”
  • “Flag any content that appears export‑controlled.”

Quality checks

  • “Provide a checklist of risks and assumptions.”
  • “Highlight contradictions between sources.”

Review before send

  • “Produce a final version and a separate ‘review notes’ section.”
Evaluate outputs →
Quality bar

Evaluate results with a quick rubric

Accuracy

  • Facts match sources; citations present
  • Numbers add up; no contradictions

Relevance

  • On goal, on audience, scoped to task
  • Unnecessary sections removed

Clarity

  • Plain language; skimmable structure
  • Actionable next steps & owners
Avoid these patterns →
What to avoid

Prompt anti‑patterns

Vague asks

“Do something with this.” → Specify goal, audience, and format.

Source‑less tasks

Expecting specific answers without providing documents, tables, or context.

Overlong prompts

Walls of text hide the goal. Use headings and bullets instead.

One‑shot perfection

No iteration. Ask for assumptions, then refine.

Prompt examples

Executive summary (Word)

“From the attached brief, write a 200‑word executive summary with 3 risks and 3 decisions needed. Plain language.”

Variance analysis (Excel)

“Explain the variance in Operating Expense by department for FY24 Q1–Q4. Provide 5 bullets and a small table of top drivers.”

Deck from doc (PowerPoint)

“Create 8 slides from this Word doc for senior leadership; include speaker notes and a final decision slide.”

Thread catch‑up (Outlook)

“Summarize this email thread in 5 bullets with owners/dates and draft my reply confirming next actions.”

Meeting recap (Teams)

“Create a meeting recap with decisions, open issues, and next steps assigned to owners with dates. Share to the channel.”

Policy page (SharePoint)

“Draft a SharePoint page from this policy with sections: Overview, Who’s affected, Key changes, Actions required.”

Shortcuts & phrasing

Useful stems

  • “Using <file/page>, draft … for <audience>.”
  • “Summarize with <N> bullets and owners/dates.”
  • “Rewrite in <tone> and keep under <length>.”
  • “Convert to a table with columns …”
  • “List assumptions and missing information.”

Time savers

  • Specify audience + length every time
  • Ask for two versions (short & detailed)
  • Request a checklist at the end
  • Bookmark a starter prompt per app
